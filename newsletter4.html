Stanford's pioneering mission: Crafting super crystals on the International Space Station for Venus and beyond



Photo by Gus Mueller

Debbie Senesky, an associate professor at Stanford University, is spearheading a groundbreaking research endeavor: cultivate crystals of remarkable durability on the International Space Station, with a distinct focus on their capacity to withstand the harsh and hostile environment of Venus.

Often referred to as Earth's "evil twin," Venus presents an array of daunting challenges for scientific exploration. Surface temperatures soar to a scorching 480 degrees Celsius, and atmospheric pressures stand at a staggering 90 times that of Earth's. The vision behind this cosmic undertaking is rooted in Venus's potential as a historical analog to our planet since scientists hypothesize that Venus once had a striking resemblance to Earth, which NU Sci writer Joseph Valenti explains in a 2020 article. By delving into Venus's history, this ambitious project aspires to shed light on lessons for safeguarding Earth's environment against the grim specter of climate change.

The ingenuity underpinning Senesky’s mission lies in harnessing the unique microgravity conditions of space for cultivating new materials. In the absence of Earth's gravitational constraints, fundamental interactions between atoms and molecules are altered, granting researchers the capacity to craft materials with exceptional properties and fewer defects. One intriguing application involves the synthesis of graphene aerogels — materials prized for their ethereal weight, electrical conductivity, and thermal insulation. They exhibit immense potential as a crucial material for advanced heat shields, robust battery electrodes, and sensitive sensors.

In a parallel endeavor, California-based Varda Space Industries has embarked on a mission to enable the production of products like pharmaceuticals in orbit and bring them back to Earth. While they've launched their first spacecraft on SpaceX's Transporter-8 mission in June, Varda is still waiting for approval from the FAA and the Air Force to land it in Utah. On Oct. 23, they announced plans to land future missions in Australia through an agreement with Southern Launch, a rocket launch company founded in 2017.

This pioneering initiative represents a pivotal step towards deepening our understanding of materials science and navigating the expansive realm of space manufacturing applications. As commercial low-Earth orbit destinations continue to evolve in the years to come, these hubs are poised to serve as thriving commercial centers for space manufacturing, facilitating scientific and technological advancement on a cosmic scale.

Can the National Guard create the "perfect reef?"

The mission to create a “perfect” self-healed coral reef is something that the nation’s military has been continuously working on. Due to the impending effects of climate change, current defense mechanisms such as sea walls simply aren’t enough to fight flooding and erosion. In response, the Department of Defence has been working with the University of Miami to build a hybrid reef consisting of concrete and coral. Made of three stacked layers, the durability of the reef is supposed to reduce the energy of incoming waves by 70%.

Persistent organic pollutants (POPs): The silent killer

A silent killer is threatening Canada’s orca whales like never before. At all times, these apex predators carry with them high levels of persistent organic pollutants in their blubber. Also known as “forever chemicals” due to their long-lasting nature, these pollutants carry well-known compounds such as polychlorinated biphenyls (PCBs) and brominated flame retardants used in many electronics. Since orcas are at the top of the food chain, they accumulate the largest concentrations of these pollutants — an effect known as biomagnification. The pollution reduces the amount of fat in their bodies, which they need for warmth and energy. Furthermore, researchers have seen other serious health effects such as hormonal dysfunction, the inability to reproduce, and cancer development.

Diapers can be recycled 200 times faster with light

With more than 100,000 tons of diapers disposed annually in Germany, valuable resources such as diaper liners end up in the trash. These liners consist of special polymers that are extremely insoluble in water. As a result, strong acids were previously used to degrade these cross-linked polymers, which takes around 16 hours at 80 degrees Celcius. Even so, this recycling process is highly expensive and complex, which resulted in around 2 million tons of diaper liners ending up in the trash. Researchers at the Karlsruhe Institute of Technology discovered a way to use UV radiation to degrade the polymers in five minutes, giving way to increased efficiency and environmental conscientiousness.
