The FDA heads to the Supreme Court to defend abortion pills


Photo by Selina Banerjee, Chemical Engineering, 2022

On March 23, the U.S. Supreme Court will hear oral arguments on the Alliance for Hippocratic Medicine v. FDA case. Two years ago, the court overturned Roe. v. Wade, giving the power of maternal health back to the states, and as a result, most red states have reduced access to abortions. Things are about to change again: Mifepristone, an abortion pill first approved by the FDA in 2000, is in danger of being taken off the market. 

The plaintiff, the Alliance for Hippocratic Medicine, is composed of an umbrella organization, three medical associations, and four doctors. They created the alliance because they shared the same belief that mifepristone is unsafe and causes more harm than good. Judge Kacsmaryk and the Fifth Circuit supported the plaintiff’s claim that they were harmed: Mifepristone users who had adverse side effects racked up costs in the emergency room. They felt that keeping mifepristone off the shelves would save both time and money for the healthcare system. The alliance furthered their point by saying they were in danger of potential lawsuits by mifepristone users. 

Out of 3.7 million women who have taken abortion pills, 13 have died as a direct result (commonly due to sepsis). About 0.1% have had serious adverse effects, including infections, hemorrhages, and other hospitalizations. For comparison, the death rate for Penicillin is three times that of abortion pills, and Tylenol has more serious side effects.  There has been no written record of a malpractice lawsuit against any of the 7,600 physicians registered in the medical associations. 

For those wanting to end a pregnancy, mifepristone is the first drug of two used for an abortion. Before approving the drug, the FDA evaluated it at least four times, and it has proved beneficial, with 5.6 million women in the United States having taken it. The drug has been a game changer for some women who have had to obtain the drug via telehealth. 

The future of mifepristone lies in the Supreme Court judges’ hands. On March 26, we will find out what they decide. Some will celebrate; others will mourn. 

– Heidi Ho, Public Health & Journalism, 2027

Research Highlights


AI in everyday life: ChatGPT continues to become more prominent — both as a tool and an obstruction.

The increasingly notorious ChatGPT by OpenAI is opening a new era of digital tools beyond common search engines. But the challenges of the new tech have caused a rift in academia, with students able to pass exam questions and complete assignments in seconds. Despite the criticism of its inaccurate yet plausible-sounding responses, it has become an almost everyday tool and a second hand to search engines, changing everyday internet use.
 

Fighting mosquitoes with more mosquitoes! A wary test of genetically modified species.

Newly bred mosquitos that scientists dusted bright green were set free to fight malaria in the West African mainland. Observing the released green mosquitoes will aid scientists’ goals to replace disease-transmitting mosquitoes with genetically modified ones. As the University of California Malaria Initiative continues its research, controversy has broiled due to the unknown long-term risks of the mosquitoes’ introduction into the ecosystem.
 

The sun is changing: The reversal of magnetic poles might cause interference on Earth.

The sun is about to flip its magnetic North and South poles, a regular occurrence every 11 years. However, potential solar storms from an influx of magnetic energy may disrupt communication satellites in space, with the remote possibility of taking energy grids and the internet offline. Scientists intently observe the mysterious interior of the sun during this time to understand the enormous nuclear-powered magnet and its effects on Earth.
 
– Iba Baig, Data Science & Behavioral Neuroscience, 2026
