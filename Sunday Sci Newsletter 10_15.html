mRNA vaccine pioneers win the Nobel after years of fighting for their work

 

The Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine 2023 has been awarded to Katalin Karikó and Drew Weissman. The pair conducted extensive research, laying the foundation for the mRNA vaccine technology that enabled scientists to create potent COVID-19 vaccines within a year. In 2020, NU Sci writer Gabby Hernandez covered how Boston-based Moderna used this groundwork to create a vaccine that has been administered to over 230 million people in the U.S. alone.

 

Surprisingly, it took years before the two new Nobel laureates could convince research funders that their work had the potential to transform vaccines. The two joined forces to create an HIV vaccine with mRNA after their previous attempts with traditional vaccine technology proved unfruitful. But Kariko was told she was “not faculty quality,” which gave her no choice but to retire from the University of Pennsylvania, where the two worked, a decade ago. Weissman’s lab relied on “seed money” because he could not secure grant funding. In 2005, their paper on an mRNA approach to vaccines was rejected by both the journals Nature and Science. Still, they did not give up. 

 

When the pandemic hit in 2020, they began experimenting with mRNA for COVID-19 vaccines. In its unaltered form, the mRNA was killed by cells in test mice since their immune system saw it as an interloper. Eventually, the pair realized that cells naturally chemically alter the body’s own mRNA. They applied the same process to the mRNA used in the vaccine, and when injected into mice, the inflammatory immune response disappeared. While this method requires a few extra steps compared to traditional DNA vaccines, it ultimately produces the same result, NU Sci writer Andrew Chang explained in 2022.

 

Since then, almost one billion mRNA COVID-19 vaccines have been distributed to the public. After over 20 years of hard work and perseverance, Kariko and Weissman led a discovery that saved millions of lives.

Safety on the ice: Northeastern hockey’s pledge to player safety 

The Northeastern Men’s Ice Hockey team's next game is against the University of New Hampshire on Thursday, Oct. 26 — how does the team handle the significant concussion risks faced by our players? Northeastern University has made an unwavering commitment to protecting our athletes, encompassing the identification, treatment, and prevention of concussions. Katie Delude, the women’s hockey head athletic trainer, delves into the paramount importance of concussion management, including return-to-play protocols, as a testament to the university's dedication to player safety and the prevention of severe conditions like second impact syndrome.

From anime to reality: Japan's towering Gundam-inspired robot

Tsubame Industries in Japan is revolutionizing spacesuit design with Archax, a 3.5-ton functioning robot suit inspired by the anime television series "Mobile Suit Gundam." This colossal creation not only pays homage to the iconic '70s show but is also designed for potential use in space exploration. What sets Archax apart is its versatility, with the ability to seamlessly switch between "robot mode" and "vehicle mode," allowing it to travel at a speed of up to 6 miles per hour. How could the implications of this innovation extend beyond space exploration?

Do animals in the Savannah find human voices scarier than lions?

This recording reveals the reactions of 19 animal species to a lion's roar and other menacing sounds in contrast to a human voice. In a surprising outcome, the animals fled instantly upon hearing humans. The footage provides a unique insight into how the animal kingdom perceives us.
