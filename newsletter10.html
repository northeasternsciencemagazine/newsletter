Privacy concerns rise amid genetic testing company’s bankruptcy efforts


Photo by Michal Jarmoluk via Pixabay

Invitae, a leading genetic testing company, announced on Feb. 13 that it filed for Chapter 11 bankruptcy protection as it continues to tackle its overwhelming $1.6 billion debt. Invitae is most well known for its offerings in cancer genetic testing, as well as other disease and pediatric screening. Over the past decade, they have installed themselves as a prominent figure in the genetic testing sector and have earned a reputation for extreme accuracy in their screenings. However, many patients and providers are now voicing concerns about pending test results and the safety of their personal information after the abrupt news of Invitae’s bankruptcy. 

Throughout its run as a company, Invitae has purchased many smaller independent groups, including Cittizen, a patient information platform. Ciitizen first gained traction as a rare disease database, allowing patients, their care team, and external researchers to easily share information. This platform has been able to curate real-life cases of rare diseases, effectively helping researchers develop therapeutic treatments for uncommon diseases that may normally get sidelined in favor of larger projects. But because of increasing strain, Invitae decided to split paths with the company, hoping to improve their financial situation. 

Given the platform’s wealth of private patient data, questions have been raised as to whom this data will be shared with following the separation with Invitae. Patients are in control of uploading their documents and information as well as consenting to it being shared with researchers, but as upper management and operation changes occur, can the companies assume patients have already consented to the sharing of previously uploaded data? 

Much of the general public remains wary of genetic information and testing, especially after data leaks from companies like 23andMe. Genetic testing can offer answers and treatment options for many patients each year, but patients are worried about their privacy as corporations continue to keep them in the dark. 

– Isabelle Kessock, Behavioral Neuroscience, 2026

Research Highlights


Great apes playfully tease each other

A new study, which systematically studied aspects of humor and teasing in four great ape species, found that baby apes exhibit forms of playful teasing as early as eight months, a form of humor considered to be a cognitive precursor to joking. In the study, scientists from the University of California Los Angeles, the Max Planck Institute of Animal Behavior, Indiana University, and the University of California San Diego identified 18 teasing behaviors in the apes. They found that the apes had inclinations to repetitively display mildly harassing, playful, or provocative behavior to attract their attention.
 

Frequent marine heatwaves in the Arctic Ocean will be the norm

Armineh Barkhordarian, an expert in climate statistics at the Universität Hamburg, says the Arctic entered a new phase in 2007. From 2007 to 2021, the Arctic has undergone 11 marine heatwaves, with temperatures elevated by 2.2 degrees Celsius over 37 days on average. The most powerful heatwave lasted for 103 days in 2020. Barkhordarian’s study looking into the heatwaves also predicts a more regular occurrence of this phenomenon in the future due to increased sea ice melting in early winter. These events have been attributed to human-caused climate change, with only a 1% probability that these events were caused by another factor.
 

Physicists capture the first sounds of heat ‘sloshing’ in a superfluid

In rare materials called superfluids, heat was theorized to behave like a sound wave in addition to its normal fluid-like behavior in liquids. The theory was put forth by László Tisza in 1938 but has never before been experimentally observed until now. In a superfluid, a cloud of atoms at an ultra-low temperature flows friction-free. To isolate and observe heat moving through this substance, physicists at MIT developed a new method of heat mapping, and they were able to collect radio frequencies emitted by the substance. This allowed them to assemble footage that featured the theorized sloshing motion of heat, called “second sound” due to its similarity to the movement of sound.
