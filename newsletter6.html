Feature: Drought fueled by climate change is making West Asia’s summers unbearable

In Syria, Iraq, and Iran, a three-year drought has left millions of people with little water and resources to withstand the harsh summers. This frightening ordeal has left inhabitants thirsty, hungry, and displaced, negatively impacting societal welfare. This West Asian drought, which started in July 2020, is partly caused by climate change’s impact on the water cycle — as water evaporates at faster rates. The world has already warmed 1.2 degrees Celsius since the mid-19th century. Friederike Otto, a climatologist at the Grantham Institute for Climate Change, has examined changing parameters of temperatures, rainfall, and moisture levels in response to the severe drought conditions.

Human-caused climate change has already made life more difficult for the tens of millions of people in West Asia, co-author Mohammed Rahimi told the Associated Press. Due to the recurrence of drought, places like Syria, Iraq, and Iran have become harder places to inhabit. In addition, this issue is further worsened by degraded infrastructure as a result of civil conflict, along with weakened water management systems, co-author Rana El Hajj told AP. 

Continual burning of fossil fuels will only continue to warm the globe and increase the evaporation rates. Lead author, Friederike Otto, told AP that we’re already reaching the limits of what people are able to adapt to. Even though continual gas emissions have continually maintained high food prices, this could undoubtedly affect the livelihoods of individuals everywhere in the world.

A closer look at the rate of teenage vaping
 

Federal regulations treat vapes as aids to quitting traditional smoking. Unfortunately, many teens who have never smoked often get their hands on one — contributing to a vaping epidemic. Last year, 2.5 million teens answered in a survey that they had used e-cigarettes in the past 30 days, but this year, that number dropped to 2.1 million. The reduction in use could be explained by the ban on flavored tobacco products, which many adolescents are drawn to.
 

 

The future of mind reading for nonverbal and nonmobile individuals

 

Not everyone wants to have their private thoughts known to others, but for some people who are nonverbal or nonmobile, mind reading is one of the few ways people can communicate. In a recent study, neuroscience researchers were able to use MRI scans to link words with certain electrical signal patterns. In another instance, brain implants were used to project someone’s thoughts onto a computer.
 

 

Middle school science teacher makes science fun by exploding pumpkins during Halloween

 

Sitting through lectures may not be everyone’s favorite learning style. For his eighth grade class, Wade Curren, a teacher from North Dakota, decided to shake his class format up and teach chemical reactions through pumpkin experiments. For one experiment, he demonstrated chemical reactions produced by combining calcium carbide and water to create calcium hydroxide. When he ignited the gas, the pumpkin exploded, evoking mass excitement from his students.
