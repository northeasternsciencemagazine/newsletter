A new era of NASA echoes old hurt for Native tribes



Photo by Andreas H. via Pixabay

As one of the U.S.’s largest private rocket companies prepared its newly designed rocket for its debut launch, NASA, who funded its payload, received a letter from the Navajo Nation. The new rocket from United Launch Alliance (ULA) was launching a lunar lander — the U.S.’s first since 1972 — as part of NASA’s new moon program. The agency is funding private companies that are developing technology to help NASA return humans to the moon. But the lander had a secondary payload: human remains. For the Navajo Nation, scattering remains on the moon is a desecration of a sacred entity. A coalition of other Native tribes voiced support for the Navajo’s letter. The mission launched anyway.

In the late 1990s, NASA launched a mission to orbit the moon and map its gravity and surface. The mission also carried the ashes of the geologist and astronomer Eugene Shoemaker. After Native tribes voiced their concern, NASA promised to consult tribes on future missions. But now, as NASA empowers private companies to continue the work they’ve trailblazed, they simply have less control over what goes to the moon. The 1990 mission was a NASA payload; the 2024 mission was not. "The approval process doesn't run through NASA for commercial missions,” a NASA representative said at a press briefing before the launch.

NASA is ramping up its Artemis program to return humans to the moon and, someday, Mars, with the help of private companies, including ULA, Elon Musk’s SpaceX, and Jeff Bezos’s Blue Origin, NU Sci editor Ananya Jain explains in a 2022 article. As the agency returns to the lunar surface, they’ll again have to grapple with the principles outlined in the 1967 Outer Space Treaty: peaceful exploration for all, while avoiding contamination.

While ULA’s new rocket performed flawlessly, the lander suffered a propulsion malfunction. The craft burned up in the atmosphere 10 days after its launch.

Research Highlights

 

Revolutionizing modern science: The transformative impact of artificial intelligence

AI has quickly made its way into the workforce as a way to get a job done more efficiently. Students use it to help them with homework, and journalists have used it to generate headlines, but AI is capable of doing many more things. For astrophysicists, generative modeling, a specialized form of AI, has allowed them to research potential galaxies. In the medical field, nurses could use AI to monitor a patient’s vitals and flag a health professional if numbers become atypical.
 

Alarming uptick involving flesh-eating pathogens raises urgent health alerts

We know climate change affects our weather patterns, but did you know that it also affects microorganisms such as Vibrio vulnificus, a flesh-eating bacteria? Although rare in healthy individuals, ingesting an unlucky raw oyster can cause symptoms that can lead to septic shock and death in just a few days. As oceans warm and storm surges increase, the bacteria has been pushed from the Caribbean as far north as Connecticut, leading to the rise of flesh-eating disease.
 

Gene therapy bestows hearing ability on 11-year-old for the first time

An 11-year-old boy, born with congenital deafness, can now hear for the first time after undergoing gene therapy. Aissam Dam was born with a mutated otoferlin gene that did not allow sound to be transmitted to his brain. While most hair cells die with other mutated genes, an otoferlin mutation is characterized by long-living hair cells. Doctors at the Children’s Hospital of Philadelphia replaced his defective gene via gene therapy, allowing him to experience sound for the first time.
