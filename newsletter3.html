The discovery of introns: Sir Richard J. Roberts and his journey to the Nobel Prize



Photo by Corona Borealis Studio via Shutterstock

As a child, Sir Richard Robert’s high hopes of becoming a detective vanished the moment he received a chemistry set as a present. 

Born in Derby, England, Roberts encountered his first real mentor, headmaster Mr. Broakes, while at St. Stephen’s school. The practice problems and puzzles that Broakes provided kindled Roberts’ passion for the realm of chemistry, which placed emphasis on problem-solving skills rather than rote memorization. After graduating from Sheffield University with an honors degree, Roberts was offered a position at Harvard University in the field of molecular biology. 

In 1972, he attended a seminar at Harvard Medical School given by Dan Nathans that discussed an enzyme, Endonuclease R, that could cleave DNA into smaller pieces. After this initial spark of curiosity, Richards hypothesized that there were restriction enzymes with different splicing specificities. In collaboration with Phyllis Myers, they proceeded to discover three-quarters of the world’s restriction enzymes. However, a larger discovery was yet to come. 

Prior to the 1970’s, it was common belief that mRNA molecules were identical copies of their parent DNA strand. As scientists began examining various eukaryotic genes from animals and fungi, they were baffled at the “interruptions” of eukaryotic mRNA molecules containing unknown substances. Richards and his team primarily focused on Adenovirus-2 mRNA, and hoped to visualize these mRNA interruptions by hybridizing intact mRNA in its two different coding regions. After cloning the common leader sequence at the 5’-end, introns within the genome were discovered. Containing around 90% of the DNA sequence within its gene, most of this “non-coding” region is considered “junk DNA” that serves no apparent purpose. 

Since joining the New England Biolabs in 1992, Roberts has been appointed joint Research Director of the sector, and now works to make restriction enzyme research reagents. Having received the Nobel Peace Prize in 1993 for his discovery of introns, Richards credits his success to his valuable mentors, wife, and children who have stayed by his side.

Like mother, like child: Caecilians inherit part of their mother's gut

Baby caecilians are legless amphibians that look similar to glass noodles. They are the first species known to have inherited their mother’s microbiome. While most amphibians neglect to care for their offspring–often abandoning their eggs after they hatch–newborn caecilians are given skin from their mother. As a result, up to 20% of caecilian offspring resemble their mothers.
 

Stress and premature aging … the two are related.

Stress is part of life. We might need to tend to the mountain of dirty dishes in the sink. Or we might have to start the essay that is due tomorrow. Stress increases the level of cortisol, a hormone which breeds an environment of inflammation in our bodies and puts strain on our bodies. A constant strain on our body shortens our telomeres, a structure that maintains integrity in our DNA. As a result, constant stress promotes shortened telomeres…which ultimately leads to premature aging. 

 

Chromesthesia enhances musical works

What do Billie Eilish, Pharrell Williams, and Lorde have in common? They all have chromesthesia, which means when they hear sound, they simultaneously see color as well. Only 2-4% of people have it, and it is thought to run in families. These artists harness their unique capabilities when creating new music to share!
